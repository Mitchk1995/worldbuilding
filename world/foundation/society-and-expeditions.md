# Society And Expeditions

Status: working design, not canon.

- The center is comfortable, livable, and still culturally central, but increasingly complacent, misinformed, and blind about the wider age.
- It thinks of itself as the world's true peak, but that belief is badly outdated.
- The center also carries sticky ideologies about legitimacy, propriety, and what a respectable life should look like.
- The center maintains an official route authority, but real expeditions still depend on charter houses, sponsors, private backing, and local arrangements.
- It still tries to decide which maps, claims, recoveries, and discoveries count as official.
- Once journeys go far enough outward, many people never return and reliable information stops flowing back.
- The center therefore lives on fragments, myths, delayed reports, and false confidence.
- Truly pushing the frontier is not normal. Most people see it as reckless, obsessive, or half-suicidal.
- Major outward journeys feel less like ordinary rites of passage and more like a last celebration, a wager, and a possible funeral.
- The deepest fear is disappearance beyond recovery, not just death.
- The frontier is not only scattered adventurers. Important outer powers include hard-won city-states, route hubs, and deeper regional polities that were never truly shaped by the center.
- Mobile groups such as major crews, flotillas, caravan networks, and wandering orders matter too, but should stay secondary to place-based powers at first.
- Emotionally, the adventure should still feel personal, crew-driven, and bond-heavy.
- A recurring social contrast should be that farther places are often less ideological and more shaped by lived truth, competence, and adaptation.
